Inventory Models
Choose Your Control Model

Select the right model for your demand pattern, lead time behaviour, and cost structure.

Economic Order Quantity

The EOQ model finds the optimal order quantity that minimises total annual inventory cost — balancing ordering frequency against holding cost. It is the foundation of all deterministic inventory control.

Wilson Formula
EOQ = √( 2 · D · S / H )
D = Annual Demand  |  S = Ordering Cost per Order  |  H = Annual Holding Cost per Unit
Minimises total cost Constant demand assumption Fast-moving spares Class A items
Cost Curve — EOQ at minimum total cost

Safety Stock Model

Safety stock buffers against uncertainty in demand and lead time. The optimal SS level is driven by the desired service level (Z-score) and the standard deviation of demand during replenishment lead time.

Safety Stock Formula
SS = Z · σ_d · √L
Z = Service Level Factor  |  σ_d = Demand Std Dev  |  L = Lead Time (periods)
Prevents stockouts Service-level calibrated Vital spares priority Stochastic demand

Reorder Point System

The Reorder Point triggers a new purchase order when on-hand inventory falls to a critical level. It ensures stock arrives before depletion by accounting for demand during the replenishment lead time plus safety stock.

Reorder Point Formula
ROP = ( d̄ × L ) + SS
d̄ = Avg Daily Demand  |  L = Lead Time (days)  |  SS = Safety Stock
Continuous review Auto-replenishment trigger Lead-time aware Integrates with ERP

ABC–VED Classification

ABC analysis ranks spares by annual consumption value (Pareto). VED analysis ranks by operational criticality — Vital, Essential, Desirable. Combining both into a 3×3 matrix drives differentiated inventory policies for each cell.

ABC Annual Value
Annual Value = Unit Cost × Annual Demand
A: top 70–80% value  |  B: next 15–20%  |  C: bottom 5–10%
Pareto principle Criticality overlay Resource prioritisation Policy differentiation
